Speaker Notes

Opening: "The landscape design software ecosystem is broken, and it's broken in two very different ways."

Professional Side: On one side, professionals are stuck with powerful but punishing tools. Platforms like AutoCAD with Land F/X or Vectorworks Landmark are technically capable ‚Äî but they come with annual costs well over $1,500 and require extensive training. They treat plants as generic CAD objects, ignoring ecological reality, and force small design firms into inefficient workarounds.

The "Franken-stack": The result is a fragmented workflow: CAD for drafting, Illustrator for presentation, spreadsheets for takeoffs, and external websites for plant research. This "Franken-stack" drains time and money while increasing the risk of error.

Consumer Side: On the other side, consumers and DIY designers rely on mobile-first apps like iScape or visualization tools like SketchUp. These are easy to pick up, but they're shallow ‚Äî plants are treated as decorative clip art, and there's no ecological intelligence built in. Users quickly hit an "aesthetic ceiling."

The Deeper Questions: They may be able to visualize a patio or a garden bed, but they can't answer the deeper questions: Will this survive in my soil? Will it support pollinators? Will it thrive in this climate? Many end up hacking together designs in PowerPoint or even video games just to get the look they want.

Core Tension: This is the core tension: today's tools are either overly complex and expensive, or overly simplistic and ecologically blind. And in between those extremes lies a massive unmet need.
